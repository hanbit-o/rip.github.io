Abstract

TL;DR:

  • Traditional IL requires extensive demonstrations and unsafe exploration
  • SART learns from a single demonstration with safe self-augmentation using precision boundaries
  • Superior performance while preventing collisions and reducing human effort

Imitation learning offers a promising approach for robot training, but traditional methods often require extensive demonstrations or exploration, which can be time-consuming and potentially unsafe. This challenge is particularly evident in precision-critical tasks where random exploration may lead to collisions.

To address these limitations, we propose Self-Augmented Robot Trajectory (SART), a novel framework that enables efficient policy learning from a single human demonstration. SART introduces a two-stage approach:

(1) human teaching only once, where a single demonstration is provided and precision boundaries---represented as spheres around key waypoints---are annotated, followed by one environment reset;

(2) robot self-augmentation, where the robot generates diverse, collision-free trajectories within these boundaries and reconnects to the original demonstration.

Our approach significantly reduces the human effort required in data collection while maintaining safety guarantees. The precision boundaries ensure that the robot's self-generated trajectories remain within safe operational limits, effectively preventing collisions during the learning process.

Through comprehensive evaluations in both simulated and real-world manipulation tasks, we demonstrate that SART achieves superior success rates compared to policies trained solely on human demonstrations. These results validate the effectiveness of our safe self-augmentation strategy in improving robot learning efficiency.
